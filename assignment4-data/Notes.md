## Extract Text
Our Resiliparse-based extraction and the WET file both extract similar content overall — navigation menus, contact information, and product descriptions — but differ in cleanliness. The WET extraction produces denser, more readable plain text with no markup residue, while our Resiliparse output contains leftover HTML tags (e.g., <th id="gckmo">) and excessive whitespace, likely due to malformed HTML in the source page that the parser could not fully resolve. Neither extraction is strictly "better" in terms of content selection — both retain boilerplate navigation text — but the WET file produces cleaner formatting, whereas our approach could be improved with post-processing to strip residual tags and normalize whitespace.

## Languege Identification

Language identification errors can propagate into training data quality issues in several ways. False positives — non-English pages classified as English — would introduce noisy multilingual text into an English-only corpus, potentially degrading the model's grammatical coherence and causing it to mix languages unexpectedly at inference time. False negatives — valid English pages incorrectly filtered out — reduce data diversity and may underrepresent certain topics or dialects (e.g., English mixed with code-switching or heavy technical jargon). In a high-stakes deployment scenario, these issues could be mitigated by using an ensemble of language detectors, applying a high confidence threshold (e.g., ≥ 0.85), manually auditing a random sample of filtered-out documents, and maintaining separate pipelines for different languages rather than a single binary keep/discard decision.

Running the language identifier on 20 randomly sampled documents, approximately 3/20 (15%) were classified as English, consistent with the observation that this WARC file is dominated by Chinese-language pages. The classifier performed well on clearly monolingual pages but struggled on two cases: pages mixing Chinese and English (e.g., Chinese SEO sites with English product names), and very short pages with fewer than 10 words where the classifier defaulted to low-confidence predictions. Based on this, a confidence threshold of 0.7–0.8 seems appropriate — high enough to exclude ambiguous mixed-language pages, but not so strict as to filter out legitimate pages with occasional foreign terms.


## Personal identifiable information
Naïvely applying PII masking can introduce several downstream problems. First, false positives corrupt non-PII content — for example, replacing URL hashes, ICP registration numbers, or decimal values with |||PHONE_NUMBER||| destroys semantic information and produces malformed text the model will learn to replicate. Second, the placeholder tokens themselves may be learned and reproduced at inference time, causing the model to generate |||EMAIL_ADDRESS||| as literal output. Third, false negatives mean real PII survives into training data, defeating the purpose of masking. These issues can be mitigated by using context-aware rules (e.g., only mask numbers preceded by keywords like "电话" or "Tel:"), applying format-specific validators (e.g., checking that phone numbers fall within valid ranges), and combining regex with a learned NER (Named Entity Recognition) model for higher precision.

Running PII masking on 20 documents revealed several clear false positives and false negatives. False positives included a URL hash (80a1ad9b9f65a115...) partially replaced as a phone number, ICP filing numbers and QQ account numbers incorrectly identified as phone numbers, and an entire list of 19 European-format decimal numbers (0,7809106151) all masked as phone numbers — the most egregious error. False negatives included Chinese landline numbers (0555-5813588), 400-format service hotlines (4000 662 888), and non-US international numbers like +91 (India) and Thai formats, none of which matched our US-centric regex patterns. This suggests that for a multilingual corpus like Common Crawl, a purely regex-based approach is insufficient and should be augmented with locale-aware phone number parsers (e.g., the phonenumbers library) or a multilingual NER model.


## Harmful Content
Applying harmful content filters to training data can introduce representation bias: if the classifier over-filters content from certain communities or topics (e.g., discussions about LGBTQ+ issues or violence in historical contexts), the resulting model will underperform or refuse to engage with those topics, even when the context is legitimate. Conversely, under-filtering leaves genuinely harmful content in training data, teaching the model to produce toxic outputs. A deeper issue is threshold sensitivity: a high threshold misses borderline harmful content, while a low threshold removes too much useful text — for example, filtering medical literature about sexual health as NSFW. Mitigations include using multiple complementary classifiers, applying domain-aware thresholds (stricter for user-facing products, looser for research), and auditing filtered-out content by human reviewers regularly.


## Quality filter
Running the Gopher filter on 100 extracted documents, approximately 60–70% were rejected, mostly due to the word count rule (too few words, under 50), which correctly caught error pages, login forms, and nearly empty pages. The mean word length rule effectively removed several pages with URL-heavy content and navigation menus that inflated average word length beyond 10. However, two notable disagreements arose: a short but genuinely informative product description (~40 words) was rejected purely due to length, despite being reasonable training data; and a keyword-stuffed SEO page with ~500 words passed all four rules because its words were individually normal, even though the content was semantically incoherent. This suggests the Gopher rules are a good first pass but miss semantic quality entirely, and a complement like a perplexity-based filter would catch the SEO spam case.
